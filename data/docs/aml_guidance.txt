BANK SECRECY ACT (BSA) SAR FILING REQUIREMENTS

Financial institutions must file a Suspicious Activity Report (SAR) within
30 calendar days of detecting a known or suspected violation. If no suspect
is identified, the institution may delay filing for an additional 30 days.
SARs must be filed for transactions involving $5,000 or more where the
institution suspects the transaction involves funds from illegal activity.

SAR NARRATIVE — THE 5 W'S AND HOW
A complete SAR narrative must answer:
- WHO: Full name, DOB, address, occupation, account numbers of all subjects
- WHAT: Exact description of the suspicious activity and transactions
- WHEN: Precise dates and times of the suspicious transactions
- WHERE: Locations, branches, jurisdictions, countries involved
- WHY: Specific reasons the activity is considered suspicious
- HOW: The method or mechanism used to conduct the suspicious activity

STRUCTURING (31 USC 5324)
Structuring is a federal crime. It occurs when a person breaks up transactions
to stay below the $10,000 Currency Transaction Report (CTR) threshold.
Indicators include: multiple cash deposits just under $10,000, same-day
transactions at different branches, patterns of $9,000-$9,900 deposits,
and frequent transactions ending in round numbers like $9,500 or $9,800.
Structuring does not require that the funds themselves be illegal.

POLITICALLY EXPOSED PERSONS (PEPs)
PEPs are individuals who hold or have held prominent public positions,
including heads of state, senior government officials, judicial officers,
senior executives of state-owned enterprises, and their family members.
Transactions involving PEPs require enhanced due diligence (EDD).
Financial institutions must assess the source of wealth and source of funds
for any PEP relationship. Unexplained wealth or transactions inconsistent
with known income sources are strong SAR indicators.

HIGH-RISK JURISDICTIONS
FATF identifies jurisdictions with strategic AML/CFT deficiencies.
High-risk countries include Iran, North Korea, Myanmar, and others on
the FATF blacklist. Transactions involving these jurisdictions require
enhanced scrutiny. UAE, Nigeria, and Russia have historically appeared
on FATF grey lists and require additional due diligence.

VELOCITY AND COUNTERPARTY RED FLAGS
High transaction velocity — many transactions in a short period — can indicate
layering, the second stage of money laundering where funds are moved rapidly
to obscure their origin. Sending funds to a high number of distinct
counterparties in a short timeframe is a classic typology for breaking
the audit trail.

ROUND-DOLLAR TRANSACTIONS
Transactions in exactly round amounts (e.g., $10,000, $50,000, $100,000)
with no clear business purpose are a recognized red flag. Legitimate business
transactions typically have specific amounts due to invoices, fees, or
interest. Round amounts suggest pre-planned movement rather than organic
commercial activity.

ODD-HOURS TRANSACTIONS
Transactions initiated outside normal business hours (before 6 AM or after
10 PM) may indicate attempts to avoid oversight by bank staff. Automated
wire transfers scheduled at unusual hours, especially combined with other
red flags, should be documented in the SAR narrative.

MONEY LAUNDERING STAGES
Placement: Introducing illicit cash into the financial system via deposits,
currency exchanges, purchasing monetary instruments, or smurfing.
Layering: Disguising the money trail through wire transfers, converting
assets, using shell companies, or moving funds across jurisdictions.
Integration: Re-introducing laundered funds into the legitimate economy
via real estate purchases, business investments, or luxury goods.

SAR NARRATIVE WRITING BEST PRACTICES
1. Be specific — use exact dollar amounts, dates, account numbers.
2. Avoid conclusions — describe facts, let the regulator draw conclusions.
3. Chronological order — describe events in the order they occurred.
4. Reference prior SARs — note any previous filings on the same subject.
5. Explain what is normal — contrast suspicious activity with expected behavior.
6. No jargon — write clearly for a non-specialist reader at FinCEN.
7. Complete sentences — avoid bullet points in the narrative body.
8. Include negative findings — note steps taken to rule out legitimate reasons.

WIRE TRANSFER RED FLAGS
International wire transfers to high-risk jurisdictions without clear
business purpose are strong indicators of money laundering. Particularly
suspicious: wires to shell companies, wires inconsistent with customer's
stated business, rapid round-trip transactions where funds return quickly,
and wires to countries with which the customer has no known business ties.
